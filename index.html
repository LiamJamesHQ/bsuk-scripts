Before You Start

  1. The call won't always go in order. Random questions, tangents, whatever — roll with it. Not being in control is usually a good sign, it means they're eager. The system won't let you submit an incomplete estimate, so nothing gets missed just because the order did.
  2. Sell as if you own one. Talk from your own experience — everyone selling should speak like they've genuinely got a sauna in their life. Film your own content at the unit or at James's place if you need something to point to.
  3. Always find a way to mention mobile. It builds trust — shows we're building all over, all the time — and it opens the door to the mobile-business investment angle, which more people are sitting on money for than you'd think.
One

Open — Who We Are

What to say

"So we're a Welsh family business — everything's hand-built in Wales, made to order. We don't mass produce. We're not the cheapest out there and we're not trying to be. We build bespoke projects that last, that you only ever have to buy once."

"We've got a UK office, a team who answer the phone, aftercare's all there, and we're doing installs around the UK all the time. We actually quite often go to a client's house, strip out a sauna they had built less than a year ago, and replace it with ours."

On stock & lead time

Set the expectation early that we don't hold much stock — the range of configurations is so wide that there's rarely a finished unit sitting ready to go. It's made to order. We do build the odd unit up in between jobs, and we aim to get some nice big commercial units in, but the honest framing is: this is bespoke, built for you, not pulled off a shelf.

If they've come in off the "saunas in stock" page on the website, don't flatly say it's sold — the list isn't always bang up to date. The move is: "Let me check that one — I think someone might have actually put a deposit on it. But don't worry, if you like that design we can make it again. Is there anything you'd change from it?" That turns a dead listing into the start of their own spec.

Come and see us

The visit offer belongs in the open somewhere: "You can pop down and see us in Wales, feel the quality for yourself." Then tie it to why it matters: "You can see loads of photos online, but when things arrive they can look different — there's a lot of AI pictures out there now. When you actually feel one of our saunas, you can just tell it's a sturdy build that's going to last for years."

Even if they're way up in Scotland and it's a ten-hour trip they'll never make — the fact we're happy for them to come and inspect it says everything. If we're inviting you to look that closely, it must be good.

How to open

Answer with something simple — "Hi, it's [your name] calling back from Barrel Sauna UK" — then pause for a second. Most of the time they'll jump straight in and start talking about their project. Let them; that's rapport building itself. If they don't, come in with the reason for the call: "We've got a call booked in today, still a good time to chat? Ask me anything, pick my brains, and by the end of the call you'll have a fully itemised estimate sent through to your email." That gives the call a clear purpose.

Get them talking about themselves early — their land, their plans, the neighbour's farm, whatever it is. People warm up fast when you're genuinely interested, and it tells you where they are in the process.

The Welsh spiel — hand-built in Wales, not the cheapest, buy it once — has to land somewhere: start, middle, end, or twice over in different ways. The one place it can't land is straight after the price. Say it before, never after, or it sounds like you're making excuses for the number.

The move
Set the quality frame before price ever comes up. Everything after this defends it — made in Wales, come and see it, buy once. Made-to-order isn't a drawback — it's proof it's bespoke.
Two

Read the Customer

What to say / listen for

Gauge budget from how they phrase it. "I don't want anything massive, just something for two to four" — they're working to a budget, hold steady, don't push. "There's two of us but we'd love friends and family over" — budget's there, push the size up.

Ask capacity straight out: "Roughly how many people are you thinking, at any one time?" That answer is your cue. If it's vague or small, that's when the icebreaker line lands — "are they bodybuilders or ballerinas? It's a hard one" — gets a laugh, gets you an honest answer. It's a response to their capacity answer, not a cold opener.

For commercial, once capacity comes up, make the go-bigger case: picture a nice big sauna with only four people in it — spread out, loads of space, lying down both sides. Now picture six people inside and twenty being turned away outside. Which would you rather? There's no harm going bigger — fewer people just means more space, and it's also where you get a read on what they can actually afford.

When they give you a capacity, that's also the natural moment to recommend a changing room — not just somewhere to get changed, but an airlock (see Card 4). Frame it as "for that number, I'd include a changing room, and here's why."

The move
Diagnose before you pitch. You're not selling size — you're reading them and nudging toward the right fit.
Three

Diameter — 2m vs 2.3m

What to say

"Standard diameter's 2 metres. But going up to 2.3, it's surprising how much more space you get." Talk from your own body if it fits, or a friend or family member's if you're not built for it — "my mate's six foot three and pretty broad, sat in the 2.3 he's got plenty of room, head's not touching the sides, he can lie flat on the bench without his arm dropping off the edge." Benches are wider, backrests thicker, more space between them.

"Don't worry about heat-up time — you match the heater to the room, so a bigger room just gets a bigger heater. Same as a bigger radiator. Makes barely any difference."

On value: "Look, we don't compete on price with the really cheap saunas — they're selling for less than ours cost us to make. But once you've got a larger sauna, we try to put a ceiling on what going wider actually costs you. We're not really making money on it, we just want to give good value to someone who's already investing and wants to use the space properly." Think of it as a loss leader — you're glad they're doing business with you, so you're not going to sting them for a bit more width. Widening is much cheaper than lengthening, so it's the better-value way to get more room.

The move
Your own body in the sauna is the whole pitch — or a friend or family member's if you're not the size for it. Genuine enthusiasm does the selling — lean into it.
Four

Length & Changing Room

What to say

"Three metres is a really nice size — you can lie down either side with space at the end for a bucket and ladle at your feet. And with a changing room it's more than just changing — you can chill between sessions, take a break."

Spec rule — never get this wrong: the changing room comes out of the total length, it's not added on top. A 3m sauna with a 1m changing room is a 2m hot room. Quote it that way from the start, or the customer ends up with a smaller sauna than they pictured.

"Sat in a sauna with the door flying open every five minutes, gust of cold coming in — it's jarring. That changing room's an airlock, a heat trap. The second door's sandblasted glass so it opens silent. Far more relaxing for everyone inside."

Play the changing room down, don't oversell it. The line, exactly: "All you really need is a one-metre space — enough for a buffer, and four people could still sit in there." If you keep piling the selling points on, it starts to feel like an oversell — and really it's just the partition sitting at a different point. If they want a bigger changing room, they'll say so.

"We can build under-seat storage in there too — wood on the left, towels and water on the right. And with a wood burner on the left as you walk in, you grab a log from under the seat without even stepping into the hot sauna."

"And we can fit plug sockets in the changing room too — especially handy with the solar systems. Charge your phone, plug bits in, all in there."

The porch is an easy add here too — £450. Sounds cheap against everything else, and it usually is the easiest yes on the whole call: somewhere to sit out front, rest between sessions.

Commercial vs residential — same point, different angle

Commercial — lay the jarring point on thick. It's about their customer's experience, not theirs: "When your clients are sat inside and that door's opening and closing straight to the outside, it's jarring. With the changing room, the second door's silent — it's just a much nicer overall experience for your clients." They hear "my clients" and it sells itself.

Residential — go personal instead. The ritual story below does the work. You can still drop the personal story into a commercial call too — it just shouldn't be the lead.

The move
Turn "I'll get changed elsewhere" into "actually that makes sense." Storage plus heat trap stacks reasons to go bigger — but the one-metre line keeps it feeling like advice, not a push.
Example anecdote

Talk them through your own ritual — fifteen minutes in the heat, then into the changing room, crack the door, catch your breath, back in, back out. Afterwards you might sit out there thirty to forty minutes just relaxing — cool blue lights under the seats in the changing room, warm ones in the sauna. Proper hot-and-cool vibe.

Example anecdote — the VAT one (business owners)

There was a customer who ordered a two-metre changing room with a table and chairs in it, sauna on the end — and put the whole thing through his business as a home office so he could claim the VAT back. And fair enough — it is a home outdoor office, there just happens to be a heater in there. Good one to throw in when you know it's a business owner — frame it as you trying to help them save, working with them, rather than charging thousands on top to hand to the government. Give them the idea, let them run with it.

Five

Lighting

What to say

"The undersea warm LED lights give off a really nice ambience — nothing overpowering, it's all about the glow. We fit them under the seats, and we've started doing them behind the backrests too."

Then talk them out of the wall lights: "Realistically you don't really need the wall lights in the main sauna. It's nice to sit with your back to that wall and your feet facing out, and a wall light there stops you doing that. The undersea LEDs give off plenty on their own."

"And these aren't random LEDs stuck under a seat — heatproof, sauna-rated, proper trunking, a wooden frame round them, fixed securely. Designed for saunas."

"We recently did cool blue lights in the changing room and warm undersea LEDs in the sauna — lovely hot-and-cool vibe. You step into the changing room, open the second door, and you really feel yourself cooling off before going back in. Therapeutic in itself."

On the electrics — fully built vs built on site

Fully assembled delivery: "The whole unit arrives with the switches fitted, the lights all wired through — ready to plug and play with a caravan-style electric plug." That's a real benefit of taking it fully built, so sell it as one — everything's done by our electrician-qualified team member at the workshop before it leaves.

Built on site: "Our carpenters will put everything in place — switches, heater, wiring routed — and leave it ready for your electrician to make the final connection to the mains. That's standard for any hardwired install." Don't get into who on the team can or can't touch electrics — the framing is simply: workshop builds and mobiles are done by our electrician-qualified team member; on-site builds, your own electrician does the connection.

Mobile saunas are different again — fully turnkey, professionally tested and wired, with the battery and solar already fitted and ready to go.

And mention the manual — everything they need on the lighting and electrics is written up and comes with the sauna, so nobody's left guessing after the install.

The move
Sacrificing the wall lights buys trust for everything else. The blue-light story sells the changing room without asking for it. And "arrives plug-and-play" quietly sells the fully built delivery — which is the delivery we want them to pick.
Six

Bench Layouts

What to say — U-shaped

"Personally I much prefer the heater on the side — it's a design we came up with, not traditional, but when you walk in all you see is the view, especially with a flat glass or globe window. You can lie right at the end looking out. With straight benches either side you've got the heater in the middle, and if it's a wood burner the chimney's up through your view and the safety rail eats your space."

"We can fit grooves into the seats facing each other, so you can join the two sides into one interchangeable middle section, built from the same smooth aspen — great for stretching, hot yoga, or just sprawling out."

What to say — L-shaped & the rule

"L-shape's for the compact — heater on the left as you walk in, makes the most of the space. Works nicely with a glass door front so you can sit in the middle looking straight out."

"You can only do L-shape with an electric heater. A wood burner needs to go in the middle at the end with benches either side — not enough space otherwise."

The move
"Personally I prefer" lets them feel advised, not sold. If they want the other layout you've lost nothing — you agree and it's their idea.
Example anecdote one — the grandad

There's a customer whose grandad had the dome-window sauna — they'd put a mattress in and the grandkids would have campouts in there, the dome basically a viewing window for the stars. It doubled up as a glamping pod. Lovely one to tell when they've got kids or grandkids — paints such a nice picture that sometimes the story alone sells the whole idea.

Example anecdote two — the dog's box

One fella said he ends up sleeping out in the sauna whenever he's in the doghouse with the wife — his own words, "the dog's box." Gets a laugh, and it quietly makes the point that with the interchangeable middle in, it's genuinely comfortable enough to sleep on.

Example anecdote three — locked out

One of the team never bothered fitting the interchangeable middle to his own sauna — then came home off a night out, lost his keys, and ended up sleeping in the sauna waiting for the locksmith, lying there thinking "why didn't I fit the middle section." It was the first thing he did when he got round to it. Good one for the lads — if you ever lose your keys, there's your spare bed.

Three stories, one point — pick the one that'll land best with the person on the phone. Grandkids get the stars, couples get the dog's box, the lads get locked-out.

Seven

Windows

What to say

Always diagnose first: "Where's the sauna going — flush against a wall, or have you got some nice views looking out?" If it's against a wall, don't pitch a window at all. If there's a view, let them talk about it — ask about the view, the space, mention we've done something similar elsewhere, and let them enjoy talking about their own place for a bit before you pitch anything.

Then bring in the globe: "Have you seen our panoramic dome windows? We get a timber ring cut at a specialist sawmill just for us, custom moulded, frames the whole inside, and we build the benches flush into the dome so you're basically sat inside it. Especially for commercial, it's a real wow factor — it's quirky enough that people take photos and videos and tag you in them, so it does a bit of organic promotion on its own. Nice mix of classic sauna and a modern twist."

"If you want to keep costs down, the full flat glass is also really nice — we build a floating bench for it. And the half-moon's still lovely, gives you your view at a lower price point. If they can afford the globe, they'll go for the globe — no need to push it too hard."

Spec rule — never get this wrong: the external-feed wood burner ONLY pairs with either the two double-glazed windows or the half-moon — never the big flat glass. You're not passing logs through a sheet of glass. With the half-moon, the chute runs through the wooden bottom half and the top half is the window. All windows are double glazed. Check the combination before you quote it — this is the one that keeps getting forgotten.

The move
The diagnostic question stops you selling a window to a blank wall — and gets them talking about their view, which they love. Not pushing the globe is the trust move.

Eight

Heaters

What to say

Start on the fence: "There's pros and cons to each." Wood burner: "It's lovely and ambient — the glow, seeing the fire. There's something very natural and therapeutic about it, almost primal." It also makes great content — a video of the fire going, zooming out to the sauna, is different to just another photo post.

With any wood burner, we include all the safety accessories as standard — base plate, heat shields, guardrail/safety rail, shields on top and around the flue. Worth saying out loud so they know it's all covered, not extra.

Electric WiFi: "Especially the WiFi model — connects to an app on your phone via Harvia, turn it on and off remotely, set timers. For a commercial with electric access it's less maintenance. But if you've got cleaners turning an Airbnb over daily, getting the fire going is one more easy job for them." The Harvia heaters are shortlisted too — single-phase or three-phase depending on their supply, so it's one quick question, not a catalogue.

When they lean electric, nudge them over the line: "I mean, realistically — what are you more likely to do every day, start a fire or press a button on your phone? You'll probably get more use out of this one." Said that way it's their logic, not your push.

"And it's not a microwavey infrared heat where you feel zapped and out of control of it — ours use volcanic stones, so you get the steam and a natural feel, not too electric-y."

If someone's got their heart set on infrared off the back of a TikTok video, don't argue with it directly — reframe: "Look, we make everything bespoke and can pretty much do anything you want, but we've shortlisted the heaters we've never had an issue with. At the end of the day: a cheaper heater with a switch, a nice-looking one you control with an app, a wood burner you can watch the flames in, or one with an external chute so you feed logs from outside — we've shortlisted one of each, so there's no need to pick from a hundred options." That kills choice paralysis and almost always lands on one of the four.

Heater on the side, as you walk in, is worth mentioning too — nobody's walking past a row of sweaty bodies to feed logs at the far end, you're stood right by the changing room, and it gives you a natural reason to check in on everyone as you pass.

Then back them the moment they lean: "Sounds like the wood burner's the best fit for you, honestly."

The move
Stay on the fence until they choose — then it's always their decision. The volcanic-stones line rescues electric from feeling second-rate, and the four-way shortlist turns endless choice into an easy one.
Straight answer — the external-feed trade-off

If they ask whether you can see the flames from inside on an external-feed burner: you can't — that's the trade-off, and it's a bit annoying. Be upfront: "We've spoken to Harvia about making one with a viewing window in the feed itself, and they agree it should exist — but right now it's just not a function. External access or flames in view, you pick one." Honesty here buys more than a workaround would.

Example anecdote — you're not waiting long

Paint the picture that there's no long wait to use it. Lights on, oils out — as soon as it starts getting warm you can be in there having a lovely warm aromatherapy session while it climbs. Within about half an hour the stones are hot, one ladle of the oil water on, and three seconds later you've got a wall of heat right to your bones. The point of the story: you don't have to wait that long — before you know it, you're off.

Nine

Mobile Saunas

Why you always mention it

Mobile isn't just a product pitch — it's a trust-builder. Bringing it up highlights that we're bespoke manufacturers who can do a wide range of things, not a shop selling one fixed item. And it's not commercial-only: plenty of residential and personal-use clients want it too. Someone living on a farm might want to move it around their estate, sauna in different spots. So working mobile into any call — even a straight residential one — quietly explains who we are and what we're capable of.

What to say

"These trailers are built bespoke to order in our fabrication workshop in Wales. You can buy off-the-shelf trailers cheap, but nothing off the shelf is designed to be part of a mobile sauna — the suspension, the wheels, the galvanised steel reinforcement, all built for purpose. It gets tested at DVSA and it's on record as a mobile sauna trailer — there are no other trailers in existence with that."

"And we don't just plonk a sauna on a trailer — we fix it to buffalo boards, L-brackets under each leg, then box it in with a timber decking platform and build the steps from the same material. So it looks like a nice raised deck, not a sauna on a random trailer. And there's something quite nice about walking up steps into a sauna too — it elevates your view once you're in there."

"They're usually off-grid — we can fit a full solar system for all the lighting, plus plug sockets inside for charging your phone. We wire the whole lot so it arrives fully ready to go. It's a turnkey solution — you could start your business straight away." Use those words — "turnkey solution" is the phrase that makes them picture opening next month.

"There's a chart on the website showing the right vehicle for each size. And just to put it in context — these are legal requirements, it's not that the car will struggle. James moved one of these two-tonne saunas across a drive by hand the other day — mind you, he's over 100 kilos and six foot three, so not saying you do that — point is it's not going to rip your car in half moving it over your drive." There's a video of it — good one to send after the call.

If someone asks the price without the trailer, give it — but explain why it's not really the comparison they think it is: "It's not just a trailer. You'd buy a trailer for X, pay someone to lift the sauna onto it, then pay a carpenter — could easily be fifteen hundred quid — to box it in with decking. And at the end of it you've got a random trailer that was never designed to carry a sauna in the first place." That's usually when the penny drops.

What to say — pushing toward mobile now

For anyone who's ready to get going — land bought, just wants to move — it's worth the "ask forgiveness, not permission" angle: getting a mobile sauna means they don't have to wait out a long planning process to start using the space.

On councils: they're generally on side with wellness — much easier sell than, say, a bar. What you're not in control of is how long they take to say yes, which is exactly the case for getting a mobile sauna now and starting straight away rather than waiting on planning.

Once it's parked up, there's nothing stopping a bamboo wall and a bath going in around it either — quickly looks like a proper outdoor wellness space. But the fence comes down in a day, the ice bath goes in a van, and the sauna gets wheeled away, if they ever need to.

The move
DVSA plus "no other trailer has this" is a hard trust anchor. The pushing-it-by-hand joke defuses towing fear without giving licence advice — point them to the website chart, never advise on licences yourself.
Example anecdote — the big one

There's a guy in Devon — "Escape to the Sauna" — put his life savings in, tested two or three spots a week, Monday–Tuesday one place, Wednesday–Thursday another. Now he's on his second and third units. Even once he got planning for the third, he kept it mobile — boxed into decking, raised, steps up to it, looks the part, and he can still move it whenever.

Hot Tubs

The move
Picking a favourite for them cuts through choice paralysis. The package price feels like real value against a big sauna spend — and the one-delivery saving is the rational reason to add it now, not later.

Ice Baths — The Dippers

What to say

"So there's the single dipper and the double dipper. Both come with a chiller, cooler, filter and heater machine — heats to 40 degrees, cools to zero, so it's both an ice bath and a hot tub. It's not a flick-on-and-jump-in job — you'd decide, ice bath tomorrow or hot tub tomorrow, and switch it overnight. Comes complete with thin wood steps, lids, inserts, seats, the lot."

"Single dipper's 3,995 plus VAT, double's 5,995 plus VAT."

Hold the dual function back rather than leading with it — talk about it as a lovely cold tub first, have a proper chat, and only mention near the end that it doubles as a hot tub too. Landing it last makes it feel like a bonus rather than a sales point.

"With the double you might end up in a bit of a war with your partner — they flick it to hot tub, you flick it to ice bath. Just come to a custody arrangement for the week."

"It's like a big deep solid outdoor bath tub — one head each side, go through the pain together."

For commercial, the switching time is a promotable feature, not a limitation: run a hot-and-cold day once a week or so, at set times, and let people know. Gives the calendar something to promote alongside anything else going on — group yoga session, hot-and-cold day — and gives you something fresh to post about.

The move
The switching joke turns a technical limitation into something charming instead of a drawback. Holding the function back to the end makes it feel like a bonus, not a pitch.
Live example — the man who bought two

A commercial client was being talked through the double dipper and the hot-and-cold switching days. The honest observation got dropped in: "Obviously it'd be even nicer to just have two — one set hot, one set cold — and they look great side by side anyway." He went quiet for a second and said, "You know what, I think I'll just get two." You're almost encouraging them not to buy two, and that's exactly why it works.

Flip side — read the room. If the order already has a hot tub, an ice bath and a sauna in it, sometimes you just don't mention the dipper doubles as a hot tub, in case they drop the tub. Same product, opposite play, depending on what's on the table.

Twelve

The Barrel Shower

What to say

"It's a barrel shower — looks just like the sauna, same materials, same colour, so it sits right alongside it and matches everything else. Really it's just a nice little enclosure you pop into. No door needed, it still feels solid and authentic, and it adds to that whole outdoor wellness vibe."

Uncomplicate it before they ask — people worry about heating and plumbing: "There's nothing complicated to it. No heating to think about — you come out the sauna, have a cold rinse, jump out. It's plumbed in exactly like a normal shower, that's it."

There's a tempered glass roof option too — you're rinsing off looking straight up at the sky, quite pretty.

It can also be built into the unit itself — dropped in round the back of the sauna, with the shower in the indent of the box and seats out front to rest on. When a customer's vision is built around the shower (some lead with exactly that), slow down and sell it properly rather than glossing over it — it's often the thing they're most excited about.

What to say — commercial

Push this harder on commercial jobs than we have been. It's the practical fix for a room full of people coming out the sauna and going straight into the ice bath before they've rinsed off — otherwise the ice bath ends up mostly sweat before you know it. Frame it almost as basic hygiene practice: "For a commercial setup I'd honestly make it a shower-before rule, and this is how you do it."

Cost-wise it's roughly a tenth of a sauna, so against the main investment it's a low-cost way to add real volume and finish to the space. The word is footprint: when the quote's already at twenty-five grand, a couple more thousand adds another big, solid wooden item to the footprint — the whole setup instantly looks more substantial and complete.

The move
Sell the finished look, not just the function — it's decorative as much as practical. On commercial, the hygiene angle turns it from a nice-to-have into something that feels necessary. It's an add-on, but a high-value, easy-yes one.
Example anecdote — the joke to throw in

The glass roof's lovely — though maybe don't have it in your own back garden. If you're having a proper naked shower out there, no real need for a glass roof over the top, is there.

Thirteen

Delivery & Lifting

What to say — installation

"We've seen it all — if you think your space is tight, send us photos and videos, but we can handball everything through. We bring our kit, a few of the lads come down from Wales, stay local a couple of days and build it on site. Eighty to ninety percent of the time we just come and build it there and then."

Always ask what the access is like — gate widths, hardstanding, that sort of thing. If it's easy access and hardstanding, we can bring a fully built order and just lift it into place, no build on site needed. Worth confirming early because it changes how the job runs.

What to say — delivery of built saunas

"We used to include fully assembled deliveries — crane lifting it in, the works. But clients kept coming back saying they'd found a local lift cheaper, and we were never adding anything on top. So the best way we landed on: we bring it fully assembled on one of our trailers, and you get the cheapest local quote for the lift off the trailer onto site — most people find that works out cheaper than what we were charging for the crane anyway."

"A lot of sites already have their own lifting kit, some farms too — and our guys are experienced, they'll be there to oversee the lift, make sure the straps are on properly and guide it in on site." That line handles most technical questions about the lift before they're asked.

The move
Framing the no-crane change as a saving keeps it a benefit, not a fob-off. Keep it about the saving, not about what they'd be doing anyway — that reads as accusatory.

Show Site Visits — Booking the Viewing

What to say

Set the scene properly before anyone comes down — this is the bit that goes wrong if you skip it. We're not a signposted showroom. We've got an outdoor space with tens of thousands of pounds of product on show that we deliberately don't signpost, so it's meetings only, by arrangement.

Make the "not a showroom" expectation explicit and warm — don't let them picture an industrial estate with a reception. The people who turn up frustrated are the ones who weren't told what to expect. Your process once it's booked

When you book someone in, mark the show site visit on their client card. Two messages then go out around the visit: the day before — "Still good for tomorrow? Here's where we're meeting" — and the evening after — "How did you find today? Anything you want us to price up while it's fresh?"

These are being set up as GHL automations off the booking, so the messages go for you — all you do is reply when the customer replies, and that rolls straight into your follow-ups. Until the automation's live, send those two messages yourself, same wording, same timing. Even when the yard team's given a full brief of the visit, the customer hearing from you afterwards is what makes it feel organised — and keeps the sale yours.

The move
Setting the scene on the call is what stops someone rocking up lost at the end of the wrong lane. What to say

Twenty-One

The Close

What it comes down to

Build rapport first and throughout — humour them, act genuinely interested in their stories, the field they look out on, the deer farm next door, all of it. People love talking about themselves and it's how they warm to you.

Get across it's all made in the UK, and they can come and see it in person — that's the difference-maker. You can look at photos online all you like, but touching a build in person is a different story.

On price: "People are selling saunas for less than ours cost us to make. There's no point competing with that, because ours will actually last — you buy it once."

Drop the side stories wherever they fit — they do more than any spec sheet. And remind yourself, not just them: by the end of the call they're getting a fully itemised estimate, that's the purpose you set at the open, so there's always somewhere for the call to land even if it wandered to get there.

If the call didn't go in the order of this doc, that's fine — roll with it. The system won't let an incomplete estimate through, so nothing gets missed just because the conversation did.

Always ask for photos and videos of the space, even if you don't strictly need them — of the layout, the spot it's going, whatever they've got. Two things happen: it helps everyone avoid guessing at a space from a description, and when they actually send something after the call, that's a good sign they're still engaged. It should feel like their idea to send it, not a form they're filling in for you.

On tangents: it's good to waffle. Chatting about their koi pond and going off-piste is how they end up ordering from their best friend. But keep one hand on the wheel — if it's been half an hour of their life story and the call's going nowhere, redirect it warmly and get back to the point. Rapport, not hostage situation.

Explain the order process — every single call, no exceptions

Before the call ends, walk them through what happens next — slowly and clearly, even though it's all in the link and the follow-up email. The words: "You'll get your fully itemised estimate by email, and with it a link for a £500 deposit. That deposit holds your place in the order queue — it comes off your total, and it means while you're deciding on the finer details, or just moving money around, a load of big orders can't jump in front of you and push your build date back. You were ordering either way — this just gives you a few days to get your ducks in a row without losing your spot."

Then make the paperwork side plain: the estimate is an estimate — the invoice created in our main invoicing system is the actual invoice. Nobody should be left wondering which document is which.

Why this matters: when the process isn't explained, people pay the £500 and then email asking "I've paid my deposit, when's my sauna arriving?" That confusion is entirely preventable — it means the closer didn't explain it. Being crystal clear here is part of the close, not admin after it.

Rapport, made-in-Wales, buy-once, and a story. That's the close. The queue framing turns the deposit from a barrier into protection — their place, held. Photos and videos coming back from them after the call is the real sign they're still on the hook. Full deposit pitch lives in the .

The story

Three beats — why it exists, in order

Beat one — the frame

They were ordering anyway

The deposit isn't a decision to buy — that decision's made. It's a way to hold their place while they finish the small stuff: final colour, moving money, checking a measurement. Never present it as "are you in or out." Present it as "let's make sure you don't lose your slot while you finish deciding the details."

Beat two — the mechanism

The queue is real

Builds go in order of deposit. A big commercial order landing next week genuinely pushes an undeposited build back. The £500 isn't a psychological trick — it's the actual thing that stops their September becoming November. That's why it can be said plainly and honestly.

Beat three — the reassurance

It comes off the total

It's not an extra £500. It's the first £500 of a sauna they're buying regardless. "It's not money gone — it's money off, and it buys you a few days without losing your place." That's the sentence that unlocks the hesitant ones.
