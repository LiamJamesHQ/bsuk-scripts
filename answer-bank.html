Electrics — "Do I Need Power for It?"

The answer

Comes up on nearly every call. Keep it simple:

Wood-burning heater: electric draw is very low. A standard 240V plug socket covers it — and that only runs the lights and jets, nothing heavy.

No jets, no lights: no electric required at all. Fully off-grid capable.

Solar lighting option: powers the whole lighting system — porch, changing room, sauna — off-grid, no mains, zero running cost.

Electric WiFi heater (Harvia, app control) does need mains access. Better fit for commercial/Airbnb where lighting a fire daily is a chore.

Word for word

"Say you've got a woodburning heater — the electric's very low, standard plug socket, and that just handles the lights and jets. Build it without those and there's no electric needed at all."

Base, Siting & Groundworks

"What do I stand it on? Do I need to prep?"

Any solid, level surface works — patio, concrete pad, gravel, hardstanding. As long as it's level, they're good. No need to lift an existing patio.

The sauna sits on horizontal legs/feet with deep grooves the barrel shape starts from — cut precise to form the barrel. The weight packs it right down onto the base.

Railway sleepers are the cheapest leveller — dig them in where the feet sit, done. On an existing slab: build straight on it if level, sleepers under the feet if any levelling's needed.

Confirm with Liam (1) Whether free sleepers are standard or case-by-case before promising them — James had a large batch in and included them free on a recent job. (2) Exact feet-spacing measurements per length — James was sketching these for a live client with sleepers; grab those sketches for the content library.

On-Site Build vs Delivery

The cost difference (Tania asked this directly)

Delivered fully assembled is +£495. Full on-site build is +£1,250 (£1,500 for large). So on-site build runs roughly £755–£1,005 more than assembled delivery, depending on size.

Frame it right: on-site build is for when access won't allow a pre-built unit in — not the default. If access is easy and there's hardstanding, we bring it fully built and lift it into place. 80–90% of the time the team just builds it on site anyway.

Cross-ref: Card 13 (Delivery & Lifting) — ask access early, it changes how the job runs.

Nine

Heaters — Wood vs Electric

The quick facts

Wood burner: ambient, the glow, natural and therapeutic. All safety accessories included as standard — base plate, heat shields, guardrail, flue shields.

Electric WiFi (Harvia): app control, remote on/off, timers. Less maintenance for commercial/Airbnb. Volcanic stones — real steam, not a zappy infrared feel. Single- or three-phase depending on supply.

Spec rule: external-feed wood burner ONLY pairs with the two double-glazed windows or the half-moon — never the big flat glass. You're not feeding logs through a sheet of glass.

Confirm with Liam The price gap between wood burner and electric.

The answer — handled live in the Jamie thread, reusable as-is

Yes. One sauna went to three expos before landing at a client's house.

Local move: HIAB is best — lift and go. Further: lift into a trailer and drive it. Worst case: disassemble and reassemble. Can be moved as a full unit too.

Planning Permission

The answer

Eco glamping pods: no planning permission required — permitted development in most cases. Saunas and tubs generally sit under permitted development too, but it's site-dependent.

For big projects already in planning, the follow-up should be specific: where it's at, tips to help get it through — not a generic chase.

Confirm with Liam — advisor pitch, write up separately The planning-permission partnership: Liam's met a specialist in person and partnered. BSUK can handle planning for the client, deposit comes off their build total, priced slightly under a standalone planning admin. Needs the specialist tracked down, terms confirmed, and a section built onto the site.

The Excuse Bank

Deliberate hold-backs that earn a follow-up

Park a real deliverable on the first call so there's a genuine reason to come back. Not fake — real things held on purpose. Every one lives in the content library as a native file, ready to send.

1. Shingle pricing — "I'll check what those shingles cost and come back with the pricing to add that." (The Scottish 5* castle example. CONFIRM: get the shingle video into the library.)

2. Feet-spacing sketch — "I'll send a sketch of how far apart the feet sit." James is drawing these.

3. Sleeper-base pics — "I'll dig out example pics of the base." Frame it as specific to them.

4. Access-route / delivery method — after they send the access video, come back with the delivery plan.

5. Multi-item stall — if you owe several things, make the wait the excuse: "James is sketching the spacing, I'll send it all together in a couple of days."

The move: The follow-up flows both ways — you owe them a thing, they owe you a thing (video of the space, dimensions, groundworks). Holding a deliverable back on purpose means you always have a legitimate next touch, not a limp "just checking in."
